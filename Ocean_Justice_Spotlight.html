Purpose & Context

Across the African diaspora — from the Gullah Geechee coast of South Carolina to the reef communities of Belize and the artisan fishing villages of Dakar — Black coastal communities hold generations of ecological knowledge that mainstream marine science has never documented, credited, or preserved. This knowledge is disappearing.

Where the Tide Remembers is an interdisciplinary multimedia research and archival initiative that directly addresses this gap. It documents how Black coastal communities understand, interact with, and steward the ocean — capturing their lived science through oral history, documentary film, photography, and geospatial mapping. It then builds that knowledge into a permanent, publicly accessible digital archive powered by Ocean Justice AI (OJAI).

This is not content creation. This is cross-continental archival research operating at the intersection of human dimensions of marine science, environmental justice, and community-based conservation — and it is producing scholarship, not just stories.

Phase 1 Sites South Carolina, USA Ghana Senegal Zanzibar, Tanzania Belize ★ Bahia, Brazil
Why This Research Matters
🌊

A Knowledge Crisis in Marine Science

Traditional ecological knowledge held by Black coastal communities — about fish behavior, reef health, seasonal patterns, habitat loss — represents generations of direct observation. Yet this knowledge is systematically absent from conservation science databases, marine protected area plans, and restoration frameworks. When these communities' knowledge goes undocumented, marine science is operating with a critical blind spot.

⚖️

Environmental Justice Is a Scientific Problem

Black coastal communities face disproportionate exposure to climate-driven habitat loss, industrial overfishing, and exclusion from conservation decision-making — yet are rarely centered in marine research. This project generates the documented evidence of those inequities while simultaneously demonstrating that these same communities are active conservation agents, not just subjects of environmental harm.

Marine Science Components

The Science at the Core of This Work

Where the Tide Remembers is grounded in the emerging field of human dimensions of marine science — the study of how people understand, relate to, and shape ocean ecosystems. This project contributes rigorous, community-engaged primary research across three scientific domains:

01 / Conservation Science

Community-Led Conservation

Documenting conservation practices developed by Black coastal communities — fishing rotations, reef stewardship, seasonal restrictions — that function as de facto marine protected area management. This validates community science as legitimate conservation data.

02 / Restoration Ecology

Community-Based Restoration

Capturing participation in and knowledge of local habitat restoration — including mangrove recovery, reef rehabilitation, and seagrass protection — across diaspora communities with generational relationships to these ecosystems. Connects local practice to global restoration science.

03 / Science Communication

Community Science Events

Ocean knowledge workshops and public screenings at each site serve as formal science communication events — translating ecological data and conservation challenges into accessible dialogue, building two-way exchange between researchers and communities.

Platform Technology

Ocean Justice AI (OJAI): A Research Platform, Not Just a Website

OJAI is the technological spine of this project. It functions as an interactive, map-based digital archive that geographically embeds every oral history, film, photo essay, and environmental data point collected across the six research sites.

Where most research archives are static repositories, OJAI is designed as a living system — built to expand across phases and geographies, to integrate new environmental data, and to serve simultaneously as a research database, a public education tool, and an advocacy platform for ocean justice.

OJAI represents what AI-assisted archival work can look like when it centers communities of color: using technology not to replace human knowledge, but to make it visible, navigable, and permanent.

📍

Collaboration Interest · Duke University

A Natural Bridge to Dr. Brian Silliman's Work at Duke

This project sits at a direct intersection with the research agenda of Dr. Brian Silliman, Rachel Carson Distinguished Professor of Marine Conservation Biology at Duke University's Nicholas School of the Environment, and lead investigator of Duke RESTORE.

Dr. Silliman's work centers on coastal ecosystem conservation and restoration — including coral reef systems, mangrove communities, seagrass beds, and the role of positive species interactions in recovery. His lab has active Caribbean field research, has received funding to assess blue carbon in Belize mangroves, and Duke RESTORE is currently conducting GIS mapping of coral restoration sites across the Caribbean. These are precisely the ecosystems and the geographies at the heart of Where the Tide Remembers.

What Cherryia brings that lab-based restoration science does not: the human layer. The communities living adjacent to Belize's barrier reef and Caribbean mangrove systems hold decades of observational data about ecosystem change — data that no instrument has captured. An oral history and archival methodology, combined with geospatial mapping via OJAI, could directly complement the ecological field data Dr. Silliman's team generates.

The convergence is clear: conservation ecology + community knowledge systems + Caribbean ecosystems. A summer research collaboration, a joint Belize fieldwork component, or a PhD trajectory through Duke's Nicholas School would allow this work to evolve from archival research into peer-reviewed marine conservation science — grounded in both ecological rigor and human dimensions.
