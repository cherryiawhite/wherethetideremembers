Purpose & Context

Across the African diaspora — from the Gullah Geechee coast of South Carolina to the reef communities of Belize and the artisan fishing villages of Dakar — Black coastal communities hold generations of ecological knowledge that mainstream marine science has never documented, credited, or preserved. This knowledge is disappearing. The communities that carry it are on the frontlines of climate change: the very crisis produced by the same colonial-capitalist systems that historically dispossessed them and forced their intimate relationship with the coast.

Where the Tide Remembers is an interdisciplinary multimedia research and archival initiative documenting how Black coastal communities understand, interact with, and steward the ocean. It captures their lived science through oral history, documentary film, photography, and geospatial mapping — then builds that knowledge into a permanent, publicly accessible digital archive powered by Ocean Justice AI (OJAI).

This is not content creation. This is cross-continental archival research at the intersection of human dimensions of marine science, environmental justice, and oral history ethnography — grounded in communities, built with communities, and returned to communities. It names and begins to fill an emerging field: Black Ocean Knowledge Systems.

Phase 1 Sites South Carolina, USA Ghana Senegal Zanzibar, Tanzania Belize Bahia, Brazil
Why This Research Matters

A Knowledge Crisis and a Climate Crisis — Inseparable

The communities most dispossessed by colonial history are the communities most exposed to climate change — and they hold the ecological knowledge marine science most urgently needs.

The Knowledge Gap

Black Ocean Knowledge Is Disappearing

Traditional ecological knowledge held by Black coastal communities — fish behavior, reef dynamics, seasonal migration patterns, habitat change across generations — is systematically absent from conservation databases, marine protected area plans, and restoration frameworks. When this knowledge goes undocumented, marine science operates with a critical blind spot. This project fills it before it becomes irreversible.

The Justice Dimension

Environmental Justice Is a Scientific Problem

Black coastal communities face disproportionate exposure to climate-driven habitat loss, industrial overfishing, and exclusion from conservation decision-making — while simultaneously practicing community-led conservation that science has not credited. Documenting this is both a justice intervention and a scientific contribution. In the framework of colonial ecology, the two cannot be separated.

Marine Science Components

The Science at the Core of This Work

Grounded in human dimensions of marine science, this project contributes rigorous, community-engaged primary research across six linked scientific domains.

Fisheries Science

Artisan Fishing & Ocean Knowledge

Intergenerational knowledge of fish behavior, seasonal migration, habitat associations, and population change across six Atlantic and Indian Ocean communities — creating ecological baselines that instrumental monitoring cannot provide retroactively.

Restoration Ecology

Community-Based Restoration

Community participation in and observation of habitat restoration — coral reef rehabilitation in Zanzibar and Belize, mangrove protection in Senegal, oyster reef rebuilding on the Gullah Geechee coast — connecting local practice to global restoration science.

Science Communication

Community Science Events

One structured event per site — ocean knowledge workshops, public screenings, youth sessions, story circles — functioning as formal science communication and two-way knowledge exchange between researchers and communities.

Climate Science

Climate Vulnerability Documentation

Observational records of ecosystem change across generations — coral bleaching, fish stock collapse, sea level rise, salinity intrusion — that far predate scientific monitoring in these regions. This archive captures the long baseline formal science lacks.

Linguistics & Ecology

Language as Ecological Data

Wolof and Lebou fishing vocabularies, Swahili maritime terminology, Gullah tidal language, and Candomblé ocean cosmology each encode ecological relationships accumulated over centuries. OJAI preserves these as marine scientific data in their own right.

Geospatial Research

Environmental Data Integration

Every story geocoded and overlaid with sea level measurements, reef health indices, and fisheries stock assessments — making community knowledge and scientific data directly comparable within OJAI’s interface for the first time.

Theoretical Framework

Dunnavant, Crichlow, and Gilroy in Conversation

This project sits at the convergence of three major intellectual traditions, each contributing a distinct analytical lens that together constitute its scholarly foundation and position it within the field of decolonial environmental humanities.

01 — Dunnavant

Colonialism as Ecological Project

Slavery reshaped ecosystems. Enslaved Africans were ecological engineers whose embodied knowledge — tidal hydrology, estuarine ecology, rice cultivation — built colonial agriculture. This project extends that framework to the ocean: the Atlantic was the ecological infrastructure of the diaspora, and its coastal communities still carry both the knowledge and the cost.

02 — Crichlow

Climate as Colonial Raciality

Crichlow’s Climate Change, Decolonization and Global Blackness Lab argues that climate catastrophe cannot be separated from 500 years of colonial modernity. Her concept of colonial ecology — the inseparability of nature’s domination from peoples’ domination — is empirically embodied at every site in this archive.

03 — Gilroy

The Black Atlantic as Living Ecosystem

Gilroy theorized the Atlantic as the medium of Black cultural formation. This project makes that Atlantic a physical and ecological object of study. OJAI maps the transatlantic connections — making the ecological relationship between diaspora sites visible as navigable, geocoded data.

04 — Maritime Archaeology

Waterscape as Archive of Labor

Maritime archaeology reads waterscapes as archives of labor, power, and resistance. The tidal rice fields of South Carolina, reef navigation knowledge of Swahili communities, and dike systems of West African origin are archaeological objects as much as they are living practices still held in community memory.

Deliverable Media & Methods

What This Project Produces

Each deliverable functions as both a standalone output and a component of the living OJAI archive — every story, film, and photograph mapped geographically and made permanently accessible.

01 — Oral History
Recorded Interviews

Five voices per region, thirty total — fishers, elders, conservationists, and community leaders. Audio and video recorded, transcribed across community languages, and archived with geocoded coordinates in OJAI.

02 — Documentary Film
Short Films, 3–5 Minutes Each

Eight to twelve films, one per community, character-driven and designed for public, academic, and institutional audiences. Embeddable in OJAI and distributable independently.

03 — Photography
Curated Photo Essays

Three to five essays — portraits, seascapes, and daily life in relationship with the ocean. The emotional core of the archive, designed to make audiences feel before they understand.

04 — Digital Archive
OJAI Platform

A live, interactive map platform embedding all stories, films, images, and environmental data geographically across the diaspora — publicly accessible, multilingual, and designed to scale beyond Phase 1.

05 — Community Events
Engagement & Exchange

One structured event per site — story circles, ocean knowledge workshops, public screenings, and youth science sessions. Co-created, not extracted. Knowledge returned to the communities that produced it.

06 — Exhibition
Public Exhibition

One major exhibition drawing across all multimedia outputs — designed for cultural institutions, universities, and ocean science organizations as a site of public engagement and advocacy.

Platform Technology

OJAI: A Research Infrastructure, Not a Website

OJAI is the technological spine of this project — an interactive, map-based digital archive that geographically embeds every oral history, film, photo essay, and environmental data point collected across all six research sites.

Where most research archives are static repositories, OJAI is a living system: built to expand across phases, integrate new environmental data, and serve simultaneously as a research database, a public education tool, and an advocacy platform for ocean justice.

AI tools support multilingual transcription and translation across community languages, thematic pattern recognition across interview transcripts, and semantic indexing of the full archive. All AI-generated outputs are reviewed by community members before archiving. OJAI returns data sovereignty to the communities whose knowledge it holds.

Geospatial Story Mapping

Every interview geocoded to exact coordinates — making the transatlantic scope of diaspora ocean knowledge visible as a connected, navigable map.

Multilingual Archive

AI-assisted transcription in English, Wolof, Swahili, Portuguese, and Gullah Creole — preserving language as part of the ecological record, not an obstacle to it.

Environmental Data Overlay

Community knowledge overlaid with sea level measurements, reef health indices, and fisheries assessments — making stories and scientific data directly comparable.

Community Data Sovereignty

Each community holds administrative access to their own stories — controlling visibility, annotating findings, and withdrawing content at any time.
